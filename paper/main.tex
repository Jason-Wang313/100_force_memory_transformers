\begin{abstract}
Contact-rich robot manipulation often contains hidden force state: latch preload, stiction, tool flex, backlash, cable tension, and relaxation histories can matter after the visual scene appears unchanged. We rebuild the generated ``Force Memory Transformers'' idea into a falsifiable evidence audit and rerun the full benchmark on 2026-06-15. The proposed mechanism keeps force-event memories, decay gates, reset gates, diagnostic probes, and tail-risk planning available to a transformer-style policy proxy. We test it on a deterministic local benchmark covering 5 manipulation tasks, 7 latent force-memory regimes, 5 evaluation splits, 9 methods, 7 seeds, and 84 episodes per method/task/family/split/seed group. Under combined stress, the proposed method reaches $0.664 \pm 0.006$ success versus $0.504 \pm 0.008$ for the strongest non-oracle baseline, online residual system identification, while reducing force-limit violation from $0.271$ to $0.231$ and damage from $0.186$ to $0.161$. Core ablations reduce success, with the best removed-component ablation at $0.628$. The correct terminal decision is \textbf{STRONG\_REVISE}: the local mechanism is promising, but the paper is not ICLR-main ready without real-robot or external benchmark validation and trained checkpoints.
\end{abstract}

\section{Why This Paper Exists}

Transformer imitation policies and diffusion-style visuomotor policies can represent long temporal context and multimodal action distributions \citep{zhao2023act,chi2023diffusion}. Contact-rich manipulation adds a harsher failure mode: the physically relevant state may be a force history rather than a visual history. A peg can look aligned while stiction accumulates, a drawer can look still while latch preload changes, and a cable can look threaded while hidden tension stores future failure.

The research bet is narrow: a robot policy should preserve action-critical force alternatives until physical observations collapse them. This is not a claim of submission readiness. The purpose of this rebuild is to decide whether the idea deserves a serious empirical continuation, or whether it should remain archived.

\section{Hostile Prior-Work Pressure}

The closest threats are not generic scene-understanding papers. They are transformer imitation learning \citep{zhao2023act}, diffusion policy learning \citep{chi2023diffusion}, learned contact dynamics \citep{pfrommer2020contactnets}, visuo-tactile transformer representations \citep{visuotactiletransformers}, conformal/risk-filtered control, online residual system identification, and recent force-aware/tactile manipulation work \citep{forcefulsurvey2025,forceattention2025,feeltheforce2025,sparshx2025}. Against this pressure, the novelty boundary must be more specific than ``add force data.'' The boundary tested here is persistent, gated force memory as a state object for closed-loop planning under hidden contact modes.

\section{Mechanism}

The proposed policy proxy, \texttt{proposed\_force\_memory\_transformer}, has five ingredients.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Force-event tokens:} signed impulses, force derivatives, release events, and overload contacts are retained as policy-visible state.
    \item \textbf{Decay memory:} force memories decay according to contact regime, preventing old preload or stiction estimates from persisting indefinitely.
    \item \textbf{Reset gates:} contact-regime changes clear stale force hypotheses.
    \item \textbf{Diagnostic probes:} ambiguous modes can trigger low-cost contact tests before committing to a high-load action.
    \item \textbf{Tail-risk planning:} the policy scores candidate actions against adverse latent-force outcomes rather than only mean predicted value.
\end{itemize}

The benchmark treats these ingredients as executable decision rules rather than a trained checkpoint. That is a limitation, but it lets us test whether the mechanism can survive strong local baselines before spending hardware or model-training effort.

\section{Benchmark}

The evaluation contains 5 tasks: peg insertion, drawer unlatching, valve turning, cable threading, and cap twisting. Each task is crossed with 7 latent force-memory regimes: stiction buildup, latch preload, backlash, viscoelastic relaxation, clamp slip, tool flex, and sensor dropout. We evaluate 5 splits: nominal, hidden-force shift, embodiment shift, delayed-observation shift, and combined stress.

The methods are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item observation-only transformer proxy;
    \item force-history behavior cloning;
    \item recurrent contact policy;
    \item ensemble uncertainty MPC;
    \item conformal risk filter;
    \item online residual system identification;
    \item robust force-threshold MPC;
    \item proposed force-memory transformer;
    \item oracle latent-force planner.
\end{itemize}

The benchmark is deterministic from a fixed seed and stores aggregate per-group metrics rather than full trajectories. Metrics include task success, force-limit violation, damage, latent-state accuracy, false memory alarms, energy/cost, regret to oracle, pairwise seed-level differences, stress sweeps, ablations, and failure cases. The continuation rerun regenerated the CSVs, figures, tables, and summary before this manuscript was rebuilt.

\section{Results}

The combined-stress table gives the main result. The proposed method is second only to the oracle and beats the strongest non-oracle baseline, \texttt{online\_residual\_system\_id}, by $0.160 \pm 0.010$ success across paired seeds. Importantly, the gain is not purchased by being reckless: the proposed method has lower force-limit violation and lower damage than the strongest baseline.

\section{Ablations}

The ablation table and Figure~\ref{fig:ablation} test whether the method is just a tuned planner. Removing tail-risk planning gives the closest ablation but drops combined-stress success from $0.660$ to $0.628$ and increases damage. Removing force-event tokens, decay memory, diagnostic probes, or reset gates also degrades performance. This passes the local ablation gate.

\section{Failure Cases}

The failure table in \texttt{results/failure\_cases.csv} identifies the weakest combined-stress task/family pairs. The recurring failure mode is stale force state when hidden load changes faster than the reset gate clears memory. This points to a concrete next experiment: learn reset dynamics from real force traces instead of hand-specifying the memory decay.

\section{Limitations and Terminal Decision}

This paper is not submission-ready. The evidence is stronger than the v3 archive because it is paper-specific, multi-seed, ablated, safety-aware, and tested against strong local baselines. However, it still lacks:

\begin{itemize}
    \item real robot validation;
    \item accepted external benchmark evaluation;
    \item trained transformer checkpoints;
    \item hardware or benchmark rollout videos;
    \item calibration of latent-force regimes from real contact logs;
    \item a complete manual related-work synthesis.
\end{itemize}

The correct decision after the 2026-06-15 continuation rerun is \textbf{STRONG\_REVISE}. The idea should not be killed, but it should not be submitted to ICLR main in this form. A credible submission would require a trained force-memory transformer and external validation against tactile/force-aware policies, residual system identification, conformal risk filters, and modern visuomotor transformers.
